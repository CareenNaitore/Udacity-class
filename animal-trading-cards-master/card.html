Tsetse fly

The species is an insect vector that spreads African trypanosomiasis. They give birth to live larvae in a process referred to as adenotrphic viviparity

Tsetse flies are exclusively found in sub-Saharan Africa between latitudes 140 N and 200 S.
They belong to the genus Glossina which is usually divided into three species groups.
The fusca group includes several vectors of livestock trypanosomes.
The palpalis group (mainly: Glossina palpalis, Glossina tachinoides, and Glossina fuscipes) mainly transmits T. b. gambiense in West and Central Africa.
The palpalis habitat is represented by hot humid forests along lake and river banks or even in coffee and cocoa tree plantations.
Conventionally, man represents the almost unique parasite reservoir, with anecdotic interventions of domestic animals (pigs, sheep, and goats).
In East Africa, the morsitans group (mainlyGlossina morsitans, Glossina pallidipes, and Glossina swynnertoni) transmits T. b. rhodesiense. Species of the morsitans group or 'game' tsetse of the savannah zones live in open woodland, bushland, or acacia thicket.
A wide spectrum of animals, notably game animals and domestic cattle, provides a reservoir of infection.
Human infection occurs sporadically in individuals coming into contact with the zoonotic cycle (hunters, firewood collectors, and tourists),but several epidemic outbreaks with a heavy toll on the populations have been observed.
